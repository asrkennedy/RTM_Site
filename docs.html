Practical Steps in Using RTM

What is RTM?

RTM stands for Risk Terrain Modeling. It offers a statistically valid way to articulate and communicate criminogenic and vulnerable areas at the micro-level. RTM is used for crime analysis and the study of global threats. This ongoing, multi-part, endeavor is being done in collaboration with internationally renowned criminologists, mathematicians, computer programmers, and public safety and security practitioners.

Projects seek to identify current limitations of risk terrain modeling (RTM) methods, to advance RTM and other methods of spatial risk analysis (SRA), and to make SRA methods more accessible to a variety of users with different skill sets. Specific studies include issues of vulnerability and exposure, evaluating place-based interventions, crime persistence, conditional locational interdependence, simulation modeling, and crime types and settings.

Why use RTM?

With the growing utilization of intelligence-led operations in the public safety and security community, risk assessments for crime and other hazards are especially important for tactical actions, resource allocations, and short- and long-term planning. Use the resources below to produce risk terrain models and maps that give actionable meaning to the relationships that exist between place-based indicators and crime outcomes.

RTM in 10 Steps

The manual method of RTM involves the following steps:
  1. Select an outcome event
  2. Choose a study area
  3. Choose a time period
  4. Obtain base maps
  5. Identify all possible risk factors
  6. Select model factors
  7. Map spatial influence
  8. Weight risk map layers
  9. Combine risk map layers
  10. Finalize maps to communicate information

Targeting Interventions

  1. The area that they focus on must be clearly identified as high risk. Veering away from these areas is fine in pursuit of crime but this should be the exception not the rule.
  2. The risk factors in the area are clearly identified in the IPIR so the intervention should focus on these risks not just be a concentration of effort around hot spots of crime.
  3. The collection of data needs to be both valid and accurate. By this we mean that the actions that are done in an area should clearly relate to the intervention strategy directed at specific risks. Also, it is important that accurate geolocation data are collected to be able to identify where the intervention took place relative to the risk
  4. There should be some expectation that over time the risk from locational characteristics should abate as a result of the intervention. For this to be measurable, a clear accounting of what the intervention actions did to reduce risk should be articulated as part of the plan based on the expected/intended spatial influence of Tx activities
  5. In looking beyond cumulative risk, we think that the intervention process can be further refined by looking at specific combinations if high risk features in the environment. These combinations, or behavior settings, can be examined u the context of interventions to see if they consistently respond to treatments, regardless if where they are located in the city. So, a strategy can be developed, based on this, that would allow for a cross city behavior settings intervention process that is not constrained by specific geographic locations.
  6. For an effective evaluation to be performed, we need to have sufficient cases in our sample to have statistically valid analysis. The "power " can be influenced by the length of the intervention and/or its geographic scope
  7. The analysis can be iterative, with the best strategies developed to have maximum effect in mitigating risks. One measure of success might be the extent to which there is a diffusion of benefits that appears in areas surrounding target areas
  8. In the intervention, there should be a process evaluation that takes place to establish whether or not the proper procedures have been implemented and that these are replicable in future iterations of the intervention.
